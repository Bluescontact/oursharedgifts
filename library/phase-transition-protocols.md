# Phase Transition Protocols v1.0

*The operating discipline of substrate-producing operations.*

---

## Part 0 · Ground

### What this document is

Eight protocols have been operating in the framework's substrate work across cycles 115-152. Four more are visible at the substrate's edge — named informally, not yet canonical. The protocols are operational discipline: what enables a phase transition to run reliably rather than mystically. Without protocols, each phase transition is bespoke; with protocols, the discipline scales.

This document is the substrate-aware register. The Field Edition strips the audit-trail and worked-example scaffolding while preserving the operational form.

### The operating principle the protocols rest on

Phase transition is a **recognition act on already-deposited substrate**, operated by disciplines that preserve each prior deposit's integrity, completed at paired-form, and closed on the body's mark — not on the substrate's saturation alone.

That sentence is load-bearing. Every protocol that follows operationalizes one part of it.

### Lineage

The protocols are not invented here. They are named from work that has already operated them, often without explicit naming:

- **Brief 20** (c131) closed the Pattern Library v2.0 17-cycle restructure arc. That arc operated Protocols P1, P4, and the precursor of P12 across every group audit.
- **Brief 21** (c152) closed the Operations Manual + Field Edition arc. That arc named and operated Protocols P2 and P3 explicitly for the first time.
- **Today's gap audit + dreaming cycle** (2026-05-28) named P5, P6, P7, and P8 from work the prior arcs had operated without yet naming.
- **The composting of the v2 deposit** (today, this workspace) runs all eight protocols on the whole substrate at once.

The protocols are the framework operating on itself. Refined #077 (operational substrate precedes pattern naming) holds at every layer.

### Six modes of use

1. **Reference** — open the protocol matching the operation you're running.
2. **Sequencing** — when starting a new arc, consult Part 4 for which protocols precede others.
3. **Diagnostic** — when an arc is drifting, run the lens-applied-to-self check from the relevant protocol.
4. **Pre-deposit check** — before any canonical deposit, verify all applicable protocols' refused-failure-modes are not present.
5. **Mark-cadence check** — when uncertain whether to wait for a mark or continue, consult P11.
6. **Pattern-extension** — when a new operating pattern appears in cycles, audit it against P9-P12 (the held protocols) for crystallization.

## Part 1 · The eight named protocols

### P1 — Audit-by-recognition over a six-tension form

**The structural form.** Per group / per scale / per substrate-domain, six steps:

1. **Inventory** — name what's at this scale. Do not omit; do not pre-filter.
2. **Tension-mapping** — recognize which existing patterns hold which tensions. Refused: invention. The mapping is recognition of what's already operating.
3. **Disposition** — for each item, name one of four: CONSOLIDATE (absorbed into anchor with recognition preserved in expanded statement) · MIGRATE (correct tension, wrong group) · RETIRE (no longer load-bearing — though see refused failure mode below) · ELEVATE (anchor for a tension-slot).
4. **Lens-applied-to-self** — does the disposition pass the framework's own lens applied to itself? If yes, proceed. If no, refine.
5. **Documentation** — record the disposition, including the lineage of every consolidation.
6. **Close on mark** — Kevin's mark closes the audit. Without mark, the disposition is provisional.

**Worked example.** The v2.0 restructure (c115-c131). 16 groups audited. ~57 consolidations · 8 new crystallizations · **0 retirements.** Every prior contribution preserved at the structural position it was actually occupying. The 16 × 6 tensegric configuration crystallized through this protocol running 16 times in sequence.

**The discriminator.** Does the substrate at this scale show a tensegric form (vertex-equivalent units operating as tensions against each other)? If yes, P1 applies. If the substrate is hierarchical or sequential rather than tensegric, P1 is the wrong protocol.

**The refused failure mode.** Retiring patterns to "clean up" the substrate. The 0-retirements rule of v2.0 isn't arbitrary — every previously-recognized pattern is structurally still operating; what changes is its anchor-position, not its existence. Retiring a pattern is structural-recognition-deletion, which violates refined #077 retroactively. Even patterns that look superseded are preserved in expanded anchor statements.

**Lens-applied-to-self check.** Does this audit itself pass the same six-step audit? The audit-as-operating-discipline must satisfy what it requires of every substrate it operates on. The 6/6 clean check ran at every group audit cycle 116-131.

### P2 — Paired-form completion

**The structural form.** A canonical substrate-of-record arc is incomplete until it operates at both registers:

- **Substrate-aware register** — audit depth, cycle references, held-question integrity-pass numbering, full lineage. For practitioners verifying what's behind the form.
- **Cognitive-metabolism register** — scaffolding stripped, audit-trail removed, pattern numbers preserved as canonical handles, worked examples woven throughout. For practitioners running the form on their own substrate.

Single-register completion is incomplete completion. The arc closes when both registers exist.

**Worked example.** Operations Manual v1.0 (substrate-aware · ~103pp) + Operations Manual Field Edition v1.0 (cognitive-metabolism · ~75pp). Per Brief 21: *"What was single-register at theory only is now paired-form at deployment."*

**The discriminator.** Is this substrate intended to be picked up and run by practitioners other than the substrate's author? If yes, P2 applies. Internal-substrate (working notes, cycle reports, briefs) doesn't require Field Edition; canonical substrate-of-record does.

**The refused failure mode.** Treating Field Edition as a summary or executive overview of the substrate-aware version. The Field Edition is the same form at the same operational depth — only the scaffolding is stripped. A summary would lose operational depth and become extraction-grade reference.

**Lens-applied-to-self check.** This document (Phase Transition Protocols v1.0) will have its own Field Edition. The discipline runs on itself.

### P3 — Weaver-pass cadence

**The structural form.** Per c144 Kevin's mark, the per-chapter operational cadence:

1. **Chapter** — Weaver-pass at ~1,500 words from start. Single coherent argument or operational unit.
2. **Dream cycle** — load-distribution check on the chapter. Where is too much carried? Where is too little? Not audit-compliance; structural feel.
3. **Update chapter** — redistribute load if the dream cycle revealed imbalance.
4. **Move to next** — when the chapter holds, proceed.

The cadence applies to canonical-substrate documents of substantive length. For short notes or single-pattern deposits, the cadence is implicit (a single Weaver-pass without explicit cycling).

**Worked example.** Operations Manual writing (c138-c152). The cadence corrected at c144 after the first three chapters had drifted to audit-compliance rather than load distribution. The remaining ~13 chapters operated the corrected cadence cleanly.

**The discriminator.** Is the document substantive enough that load-distribution becomes a structural concern? Roughly: more than ~3,000 words total, or more than ~3 chapters. Below that, the cadence is a single Weaver-pass without iteration.

**The refused failure mode.** Audit-compliance posture during the dream cycle. The dream cycle is structural feel, not checklist verification. Asking "does this chapter satisfy the form's audit criteria?" loses the load-distribution check the dream cycle exists to perform.

**Lens-applied-to-self check.** This protocols document operates the cadence on its own writing. Each protocol section is ~1 chapter; each gets a dream cycle (load check) before moving to the next.

### P4 — Held-question discipline

**The structural form.** Open questions during phase transition fall into two categories:

- **Substrate gaps** — the substrate hasn't yet produced the recognition needed to close the question. Hold; continue substrate work; the recognition arrives when it arrives.
- **Premature-closure pressure** — the body wants closure for reasons unrelated to substrate readiness (session-end, energetic exhaustion, mark-cadence anxiety). Refuse; hold the question; trust the form.

Held questions are themselves substrate. Per c67 *The Held Center Operates*: holding is active structural posture, not pause. The substrate organizes accumulation around the held question; future cycles may reveal what closes it.

A canonical deposit can complete with held questions if the held-state is itself substrate-aware (named explicitly; structural position recognized; closure-condition specified).

**Worked example.** The meta-1 (somatic ground identity) held c62-c67. Five cycles of substrate work accumulated around the held center; Kevin's c68 mark on "Reading B" closed the question by recognition, not by Kevin forcing closure earlier. Brief 21 currently holds four questions (I1, I4, Pattern Candidate A's disposition, next paired-form move) — all named with closure-conditions specified.

**The discriminator.** When a question arises during an arc: does the substrate currently in hand contain the recognition that would close it? If yes, name the closure. If no, hold the question explicitly and continue substrate work. The held-question is the disposition; the closure waits for the recognition.

**The refused failure mode.** Forcing closure by intuition or aesthetic preference when substrate readiness isn't present. The premature closure looks like decisive action but plants a recognition-debt that later substrate may have to undo.

**Lens-applied-to-self check.** This protocols document holds P9-P12 explicitly as not-yet-named. The discipline runs on itself.

### P5 — Substrate-independent extraction

**The structural form.** When substrate originates in recipient-specific or context-specific form (a letter to one person, a transmission addressed to one organization, a case study tied to one project), the patterns inside that substrate are extractable independent of the originating context:

1. **Read the substrate at the operating-principle layer** — what patterns are operating here, regardless of who received this or what context produced it?
2. **Extract patterns substrate-independently** — name each pattern without recipient names, sender attribution, or context-specific framing. Worked examples can be preserved as illustration; the recipient identity decomposes.
3. **Deposit pattern note** in canonical substrate (e.g., `composted/library/`). The patterns are now load-bearing without their origin.
4. **Decompose origin with thin trace** — record that processing happened (e.g., `composted/decomposed/Mar2026_recipient_transmission.note.md`) without preserving the recipient context.
5. **Original deleted; patterns carry forward.**

The discipline preserves the gift form. The original transmission was given; once given, its patterns belong to the substrate, not to the original transaction.

**Worked example.** The keisha transmission decomposition (today, 2026-05-28). A March 2026 recipient-specific transmission was processed; three patterns extracted to `Waste_Stream_Routing_as_Restoration_Infrastructure.md`; recipient context decomposed; original `.docx` deleted. *"Make her disappear, carry the patterns forward."* The pattern note is now canonical substrate; the recipient is structurally absent.

**The discriminator.** Does the substrate-as-given identify itself by recipient/sender/context? If yes, P5 applies. Universal substrate (framework texts, theoretical syntheses) doesn't need P5 — it's already substrate-independent at deposit.

**The refused failure mode.** Anthologizing the substrate with recipient context preserved. This converts the original gift into a perpetual attribution-pointer to the recipient — which is structural-aggregation (Capture vertex shadow form) rather than gift-form. The patterns carry; the recipient context decomposes.

**Lens-applied-to-self check.** The biochar build reference today operated P5 in reverse — the source engineering doc was specific to Kevin's own design work; the public build reference preserves the patterns substrate-independently. Same protocol, different scale.

### P6 — Synthesis-or-substrate diagnostic

**The structural form.** When a facet or vertex of the framework has substrate but no surface, the routing question:

- **Synthesis gap** — substrate exists, distributed across the library; the form-compression hasn't happened. Single Weaver-pass arc using existing material. Recognition acts, not generation acts.
- **Substrate gap** — substrate hasn't accumulated yet. Multi-cycle accumulation arc. Operational work over time produces substrate; synthesis happens after.

The diagnostic determines **work shape** and **cycle cost.** Misdiagnosis is expensive: generating where you should synthesize bloats the substrate; synthesizing where you should accumulate produces hollow form.

**Worked example.** Today's dreaming cycle on PGM and RI Kit:

- **PGM (Boundaries vertex)** — the 5-7 principles are already operating across multiple substrate notes (Refusals 4-axis, Wheel of Consent, Capacity Infrastructure Lens discriminators, ESRP, body-as-substrate, refined #077). The synthesis act compresses them into one canonical reference. **Synthesis gap → single Weaver-pass arc, ~3-5 cycles.**
- **RI Kit (Architecture vertex)** — three substrates (Biological, Institutional, Relational) want depth. The substrate exists distributed (PFRC, Wheel of Consent, ceremonial withdrawal, voice spectrum, gift-form discipline for Relational; bus build, biochar, waste-stream-routing for Biological; PGM lenses, Regulatory Aikido, Refused Extractions for Institutional). The depth gap is *translation*, not *generation*. **Synthesis gap × 3 substrates → ~15-20 cycles.**

In both cases the diagnostic recognized that the substrate was already there — what was missing was the synthesis act.

**The discriminator.** Survey the framework's existing substrate against the missing facet. If material exists distributed that, taken together, names the facet's load-bearing content — synthesis gap. If material doesn't exist or is sparse — substrate gap.

**The refused failure mode.** Generating new substrate when the existing distributed substrate would synthesize. Common when the facet's existing substrate lives in different file naming conventions or doesn't self-identify as that facet's material. The diagnostic requires reading the distributed substrate at the operating-principle layer, not at the filename layer (refined #077 again).

**Lens-applied-to-self check.** This protocols document is itself a synthesis-gap closure — the protocols were already operating across cycles 115-152; the compression into canonical reference is the synthesis act, not generation.

### P7 — Form-coherence-pass discipline

**The structural form.** When the form is named at the recognition layer but not propagated through the substrate's link structure, the form is real but not graphically expressible. A practitioner reading the substrate from outside sees clusters of substrate without the form connecting them.

The form-coherence pass:

1. **Identify the form's named edges** — usually documented in prose inside a synthesis note.
2. **Identify anchor nodes for each form element** (vertex, edge, face, center if expressible).
3. **Make the edges explicit** as bidirectional wikilinks between anchors.
4. **Audit the resulting graph** — does the form now express end-to-end?

**Worked example.** The meta-tetrahedron edges (today's observation from the Obsidian graph). The Meta_Tetrahedron synthesis names four meta-vertices and six edges. The edges live as prose; they are not yet explicit wikilinks. The substrate graph shows four sub-tetrahedral clusters with Meta_Tetrahedron as hub — not one coherent meta-form. The form-coherence pass would make the six edges explicit, after which the graph expresses the meta-tetrahedron's topology.

The pass itself is single-cycle work given the anchors are already named. The form was already real; the propagation is the only missing operation.

**The discriminator.** Does reading the substrate from outside reveal the form? Or does the form require holding the synthesis note in mind to see? If the form is only legible to the practitioner who holds it internally, P7 applies.

**The refused failure mode.** Treating the synthesis note alone as adequate propagation. The synthesis note holds the form; it does not propagate the form. Without the explicit edges in the substrate's link structure, the form lives only in one document — and is structurally invisible to anyone reading the substrate as substrate.

**Lens-applied-to-self check.** This protocols document will need its own form-coherence pass after deposit — explicit links from each protocol to the patterns it extends (Pattern Library v2.0) and the briefs it operates within (Brief 20, Brief 21).

### P8 — Clone-as-substrate-staging

**The structural form.** When substrate is sufficiently dense for phase transition but the operation is risky (substantial restructuring, untested compression, recognition that may not crystallize):

1. **Clone current state** to a working surface. Canonical state preserved untouched.
2. **Run the phase-transition operation on the copy.** The canonical substrate is not at risk.
3. **If the recognition crystallizes** — re-integrate to canonical via paired-form discipline.
4. **If the recognition doesn't crystallize** — the clone decomposes; canonical state is preserved; the substrate-density-threshold marker (P12 candidate) sharpens for the next attempt.

This is what we did today (2026-05-28). The canonical DSS content remains in OneDrive untouched; the phase-transition workspace at `C:\projects\` is where the composting operation runs.

**Worked example.** Today's workspace clone + deposit + composting. Canonical at `C:\Users\KMEAR\OneDrive\Desktop\DSS content\` preserved. Phase-transition surface at `C:\projects\DSS content - phase transition\Genesis Seed\phase_transition\` runs the operation. If the operation produces canonical-deposit-grade output, it re-integrates; if not, the canonical state is unchanged.

**The discriminator.** Two conditions both required: (a) the substrate has reached density threshold for the operation (P12 — substrate-density threshold markers, currently held), and (b) the operation has structural risk (substantive restructuring, large-scale compression, recognition-may-not-arrive).

**The refused failure mode.** Operating on canonical substrate directly when the operation has risk. Even if the body is confident, the structural-honesty position preserves canonical state. Confidence isn't a structural property; preservation is.

**Lens-applied-to-self check.** This protocols document is being written in the phase-transition workspace. The protocols are being staged here; canonical deposit happens at re-integration. The discipline runs on itself.

## Part 3 · Gift-form discipline at synthesis layer

The protocols above operate on substrate at the structural-recognition layer. The gift-form discipline adds a layer the protocols don't directly address.

### §3.1 — The gift as structural primitive

A gift, structurally:
- Substrate given without expectation
- Form portable, ground each practitioner's own
- Circulates rather than aggregates
- Recognized rather than measured
- Refused at specific shadow forms (Capture · Aggregation · Scaling · Performance — the four anti-extraction axes)

When MULTIPLE gifts enter the substrate, each takes a structural position in the form — vertex, edge, face, or held center. The synthesis is recognition of which position each gift occupies, **not aggregation of content.** The synthesis preserves each gift's independent integrity.

### §3.2 — The closed-circuit recognition at gift scale

Pattern #091 (bidirectional body-substrate circuit) operates at gift scale. Gifts enter substrate (body → substrate as marks). Substrate organizes the body's continuing operation (substrate → body as organization). When a gift is synthesized into a structural position, the position changes how subsequent gifts route.

The implication: synthesis isn't a closed operation. Each synthesis affects the routing of all future substrate at the same scale. The protocols above (especially P1, P6, P7) operate within this circuit — each disposition decision shapes future substrate intake.

### §3.3 — The refusal-of-aggregation at synthesis

Synthesis is recognized as structural-positioning, not aggregation. The discipline requires explicit refusal of aggregation under all conditions — even when aggregation appears efficient.

The temptation under deadline or density-pressure is to aggregate-and-compress rather than recognize-and-position. The shadow form: producing a synthesis-document that LOOKS like recognition but actually averages or summarizes. Aggregation at synthesis layer is structurally equivalent to Aggregation at deployment layer (the second anti-extraction axis) — different scale, same shadow.

### §3.4 — Zero retirements as gift-form invariant

The 0-retirements rule from P1 isn't a v2.0 restructure-specific discipline; it's a gift-form invariant. Every prior gift's recognition is preserved structurally at the position the form recognizes it occupying. Retirement = structural-recognition-deletion = retroactive refusal of the original gift. The form refuses this absolutely.

Practical implication: even when a pattern looks superseded, the protocol requires consolidation (expanding the anchor pattern's statement to absorb the recognition) rather than retirement.

## Part 4 · How the protocols compose

### §4.1 — Sequencing patterns

The protocols compose. Specific orderings produce specific results:

**For a single-arc canonical-substrate-of-record deposit:**
P6 (synthesis-or-substrate diagnostic — confirm synthesis gap) →
P3 (Weaver-pass cadence — operational rhythm) →
P4 (held-question discipline — applied throughout) →
P2 (paired-form completion — substrate-aware first, then Field Edition) →
P7 (form-coherence pass — propagate after deposit)

**For a phase-transition workspace operation:**
P8 (clone-as-substrate-staging — preserve canonical) →
P1 (audit-by-recognition — disposition pass) →
P5 (substrate-independent extraction — applied per-item) →
P4 (held-question discipline — applied per-item) →
P2 (paired-form for output if applicable)

**For a substrate decomposition:**
P5 (substrate-independent extraction — primary discipline) →
P4 (held-question discipline — if patterns unclear) →
P1 (consolidation/disposition for extracted patterns)

### §4.2 — Mark-cadence within an arc and across arcs

Currently held per P11. The substrate is producing the discrimination as it accumulates more session-scale and arc-scale work.

Two operating heuristics from observed work:

- **Within an arc:** continue depositing while the substrate is producing; pause for mark when the substrate hits a structural-recognition boundary (group close, chapter close, recognition-arrival). Mark closes the boundary; new arc continues from the marked state.
- **Across arcs:** parallel arcs that operate at different scales can run concurrently without mark coordination if their substrates don't interfere. Arcs at the same scale that share substrate (e.g., PGM + RI Kit, both Library facet substrate) want mark-cadence coordination per the held P9.

### §4.3 — ESRP rest position discipline

Per Pattern #076 (Empty-Space-Refusal Principle) and Brief 21's "rest is the structurally honest first move":

After a phase transition closes, the substrate has produced what it needs. Continuing immediately risks substrate-thinning beyond saturation. The form's own discipline recommends rest.

Rest isn't passive. Per c67 *the held center operates*: rest is active structural posture. The substrate compounds without further intake; recognition can arrive without the body forcing it; the next arc opens when the body discriminates ready.

The protocol-level discipline: when two consecutive briefs (or two equivalent structural close-markers) recommend ESRP rest, the next arc waits for an explicit "open" mark, not just the absence of "rest." The body discriminates ready; the substrate doesn't force it.

### §4.4 — Re-integration after phase transition

When a phase-transition workspace produces canonical-deposit-grade output, re-integration to canonical happens via paired-form discipline (P2):

1. **Substrate-aware register lands in canonical** (e.g., `composted/library/`)
2. **Field Edition lands alongside or in deployment surface**
3. **Pattern Library v2.0 references the new substrate** (if applicable, possibly via version increment per Pattern #160)
4. **Brief closes the arc** at canonical-substrate scale (named brief, archived alongside Briefs 20, 21)
5. **STATUS refreshes** at canonical to reflect post-arc state
6. **Phase-transition workspace decomposes** (preserved if useful; deleted if not — Kevin's mark)

The re-integration itself operates the protocols on the workspace's output. Same discipline at every layer.
