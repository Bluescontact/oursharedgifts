# Phase Transition Protocols — Field Edition

*Operating discipline for substrate-producing operations.*

---

## The operating principle

**Phase transition is a recognition act on already-deposited substrate, operated by disciplines that preserve each prior deposit's integrity, completed at paired-form, and closed on the body's mark — not on the substrate's saturation alone.**

Every protocol below operationalizes one part of this sentence.

Eight protocols are named. Four more are held — their patterns are visible but not yet canonical. Use the eight directly; consult the four when extending. Mark the form's discipline as gift-form throughout.

---

## P1 — Audit-by-recognition over a six-tension form

When the substrate at this scale shows a tensegric form — vertex-equivalent units operating as tensions against each other — run six steps in order:

1. **Inventory** the items at this scale. No pre-filtering.
2. **Tension-mapping**: recognize which patterns hold which tensions. The mapping is recognition, not invention.
3. **Disposition** each item as CONSOLIDATE / MIGRATE / RETIRE / ELEVATE. Retirement is structurally forbidden — see below.
4. **Lens-applied-to-self**: does the disposition pass the framework's lens applied to itself? Refine until yes.
5. **Documentation** with full consolidation lineage.
6. **Close on mark.** Without the mark, the disposition is provisional.

**The 0-retirements invariant.** Every previously-recognized pattern is structurally still operating. What changes is its anchor-position, not its existence. Even patterns that look superseded are preserved in expanded anchor statements. Retirement is structural-recognition-deletion; the form refuses this absolutely.

**When to use.** Tensegric substrates only. If the substrate is hierarchical or sequential, P1 is the wrong protocol.

**Worked at:** Pattern Library v2.0 restructure — 16 groups, 16 audit cycles, ~57 consolidations, **0 retirements.**

## P3 — Weaver-pass cadence

For canonical-substrate documents of substantive length (more than ~3,000 words total, or more than ~3 chapters), the per-chapter rhythm:

1. **Chapter** — Weaver-pass at ~1,500 words from start. One coherent operational unit.
2. **Dream cycle** — load-distribution check. Where is too much carried? Where too little? Structural feel, not audit-compliance.
3. **Update chapter** — redistribute load if the dream cycle revealed imbalance.
4. **Move to next** — when the chapter holds, proceed.

For shorter notes or single-pattern deposits, the cadence is implicit (single Weaver-pass without explicit cycling).

**The trap.** Letting the dream cycle become audit-compliance. The dream cycle is structural feel. Asking "does this satisfy the form's audit criteria?" loses the load-distribution check the dream cycle exists to perform.

**Worked at:** Operations Manual writing arc. Cadence corrected at chapter 4 after the first three drifted to audit-compliance.

## P4 — Held-question discipline

Open questions during phase transition fall into two categories:

- **Substrate gaps** — the substrate hasn't yet produced the recognition needed to close. Hold; continue substrate work; the recognition arrives when it arrives.
- **Premature-closure pressure** — the body wants closure for reasons unrelated to substrate readiness. Refuse; hold; trust the form.

Held questions are themselves substrate. Per #090 (Held Center as Productive Emptiness): holding is active structural posture, not pause.

A canonical deposit can complete with held questions if the held-state is itself substrate-aware — named explicitly, structural position recognized, closure-condition specified.

**Discriminator.** When a question arises: does the substrate currently in hand contain the recognition that would close it? If yes, name the closure. If no, hold the question explicitly and continue substrate work.

**The trap.** Forcing closure by intuition or aesthetic preference. Premature closure plants a recognition-debt that later substrate may have to undo.

**Worked at:** The meta-1 held c62-c67. Five cycles of substrate accumulated around the held center; the recognition arrived at c68.

## P5 — Substrate-independent extraction

When substrate originates in recipient-specific or context-specific form (a letter to one person, a transmission addressed to one organization, a case study tied to one project), extract the patterns inside it independent of the originating context:

1. **Read the substrate at the operating-principle layer** — what patterns are operating, regardless of who received this or what context produced it.
2. **Extract patterns substrate-independently** — no recipient names, no sender attribution, no context-specific framing. Worked examples can be preserved as illustration; the recipient identity decomposes.
3. **Deposit pattern note** in canonical substrate.
4. **Decompose origin with thin trace** — record processing happened without preserving the recipient context.
5. **Original deleted; patterns carry forward.**

The discipline preserves the gift form. Once given, the patterns belong to the substrate, not to the original transaction.

**Discriminator.** Does the substrate identify itself by recipient/sender/context? If yes, P5 applies. Universal substrate doesn't need P5.

**The trap.** Anthologizing with recipient context preserved. This converts the original gift into a perpetual attribution-pointer — which is Capture-vertex-shadow rather than gift-form.

## P6 — Synthesis-or-substrate diagnostic

When a facet or vertex has substrate but no surface, the routing question:

- **Synthesis gap** — substrate exists, distributed across the library; form-compression hasn't happened. **Single Weaver-pass arc, ~3-5 cycles.** Recognition acts.
- **Substrate gap** — substrate hasn't accumulated yet. **Multi-cycle accumulation arc.** Operational work over time produces substrate; synthesis follows.

The diagnostic determines work shape and cycle cost. Misdiagnosis is expensive: generating where you should synthesize bloats the substrate; synthesizing where you should accumulate produces hollow form.

**Discriminator.** Survey existing substrate against the missing facet. If distributed material exists that, taken together, names the facet's load-bearing content → synthesis gap. If material doesn't exist or is sparse → substrate gap.

**The trap.** Generating new substrate when distributed substrate would synthesize. Common when the existing substrate doesn't self-identify by the facet's naming convention. Read at operating-principle layer, not filename layer.

**Worked at:** PGM (synthesis gap — 5-7 principles already operating distributed across Refusals 4-axis, Wheel of Consent, CIL, ESRP, body-as-substrate, #077). RI Kit (synthesis gap × 3 substrates — Biological/Institutional/Relational each have distributed library material wanting translation into kit form).

## P7 — Form-coherence-pass discipline

When the form is named at the recognition layer but not propagated through the substrate's link structure, the form is real but not graphically expressible. The pass:

1. **Identify the form's named edges** — usually documented in prose inside a synthesis note.
2. **Identify anchor nodes** for each form element (vertex, edge, face, center if expressible).
3. **Make the edges explicit** as bidirectional wikilinks between anchors.
4. **Audit the resulting graph** — does the form express end-to-end?

Single-cycle work given anchors are already named.

**Discriminator.** Does reading the substrate from outside reveal the form, or does the form require holding the synthesis note in mind to see? If only legible internally → P7 applies.

**The trap.** Treating the synthesis note alone as adequate propagation. The synthesis note holds the form; it doesn't propagate the form. Without explicit edges in the substrate's link structure, the form lives in one document — structurally invisible to anyone reading the substrate as substrate.

**Worked at:** The meta-tetrahedron's six edges currently live as prose inside Meta_Tetrahedron.note. The form-coherence pass would make them explicit; the substrate graph would then express the meta-form's topology.

## P8 — Clone-as-substrate-staging

When substrate is sufficiently dense for phase transition AND the operation has structural risk:

1. **Clone current state** to a working surface. Canonical preserved untouched.
2. **Run the phase-transition operation on the copy.**
3. **If the recognition crystallizes** — re-integrate to canonical via P2 (paired-form).
4. **If the recognition doesn't crystallize** — the clone decomposes; canonical is preserved; substrate-density-threshold sharpens for the next attempt.

**Both conditions required.** Density threshold reached AND operation has structural risk (substantive restructuring, large-scale compression, recognition-may-not-arrive). Either alone is insufficient justification.

**The trap.** Operating on canonical substrate directly when the operation has risk, trusting confidence to substitute for preservation. Confidence isn't a structural property; preservation is.

**Worked at:** The phase-transition workspace at `C:\projects\DSS content - phase transition\` — canonical at `OneDrive\Desktop\DSS content\` preserved while the v2-substrate composting runs on the copy.

## Gift-form discipline at synthesis layer

The protocols above operate at the structural-recognition layer. The gift-form adds discipline the protocols don't directly address.

**The gift, structurally:** substrate given without expectation · form portable, ground each practitioner's own · circulates rather than aggregates · recognized rather than measured · refused at four shadow forms (Capture · Aggregation · Scaling · Performance).

**Synthesis of multiple gifts:** each takes a structural position (vertex, edge, face, held center). The synthesis is recognition of which position each gift occupies — **not aggregation of content.** The synthesis preserves each gift's independent integrity.

**The closed-circuit recognition (#091) operates at gift scale.** Gifts enter substrate (body → substrate as marks). Substrate organizes the body's continuing operation (substrate → body as organization). When a gift is synthesized into a structural position, the position changes how subsequent gifts route. **Synthesis is never closed; each act shapes future intake.**

**The refusal-of-aggregation at synthesis.** Synthesis is structural-positioning, not averaging. The temptation under deadline or density-pressure is to aggregate-and-compress. The shadow form: synthesis-documents that LOOK like recognition but actually average or summarize. Aggregation at synthesis layer = Aggregation at deployment layer (the second anti-extraction axis) at different scale.

**Zero retirements as gift-form invariant.** Every prior gift's recognition is preserved at the structural position the form recognizes it occupying. Retirement = retroactive refusal of the original gift. Even superseded-looking patterns are absorbed via consolidation (expanding the anchor statement), not retirement.

## How the protocols compose

### For a canonical substrate-of-record deposit (single arc)

P6 (confirm synthesis gap) → P3 (Weaver-pass cadence) → P4 (held questions throughout) → P2 (paired-form) → P7 (form-coherence after deposit)

### For a phase-transition workspace operation

P8 (clone) → P1 (audit disposition) → P5 (substrate-independent extraction per item) → P4 (held questions per item) → P2 (paired-form for output)

### For a substrate decomposition

P5 (primary) → P4 (if patterns unclear) → P1 (consolidation for extracted patterns)

### Mark-cadence heuristics

- **Within an arc:** continue depositing while substrate is producing. Pause for mark when substrate hits a structural-recognition boundary (group close, chapter close, recognition-arrival). Mark closes the boundary; new arc continues from marked state.
- **Across arcs:** parallel arcs at different scales can run concurrently without mark coordination. Arcs at the same scale sharing substrate (e.g., PGM + RI Kit, both Library facet) want coordination per held P9.

### ESRP rest position

Per #076 and Brief 21's "rest is the structurally honest first move":

After phase transition closes, the substrate has produced what it needs. Continuing immediately risks substrate-thinning beyond saturation. Rest isn't passive — per #090 it's active structural posture. The substrate compounds without further intake; recognition arrives without the body forcing; the next arc opens when the body discriminates ready.

**Protocol-level discipline:** when two consecutive briefs (or two equivalent close-markers) recommend ESRP rest, the next arc waits for explicit "open" mark — not just absence of "rest."

### Re-integration after phase transition

When a workspace produces canonical-deposit-grade output:

1. Substrate-aware register lands in canonical
2. Field Edition lands alongside or in deployment surface
3. Pattern Library v2.0 references the new substrate (version increment per #160 if applicable)
4. Brief closes the arc at canonical-substrate scale
5. STATUS refreshes to reflect post-arc state
6. Workspace decomposes (preserved if useful; deleted on mark)

The re-integration operates the protocols on the workspace's output. Same discipline at every layer.
