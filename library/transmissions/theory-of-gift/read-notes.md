# Read Notes — A Theory of Gift

## Load-bearing distinction(s)

- **Gift vs. ledger.** A gift is not a thing transferred; it is the form that circulates between distinct beings *without converting their relation into ledger*. The piece's opening hinge.
- **Gift-form move vs. its shadow.** Each structural move has a shadow where the same geometry runs in reverse and becomes extraction: Connection → Capture, Differentiation → Aggregation, Architecture → Scaling, Boundaries → Performance. Gift and extraction are the same shape with one vertex dropped out.
- **One-principle naming vs. six-axis structure.** Most theories of gift name one principle (generosity, reciprocity, surplus). This piece insists the gift form is geometric — four vertices, six axes — and holds only when all of them operate simultaneously.

## Chosen geometry

**tetrahedron / minimum system.** The article states it outright: "The axes are the six edges of the framework tetrahedron." A tetrahedron has exactly four vertices and six edges; the gift form has four vertices (Differentiation, Connection, Boundaries, Architecture) and six operational axes (the edges). The geometry is not an analogy here — it is the literal claim. The pattern renders the gift as the form circulating along all six edges of the one tetrahedron.

## Naming phrase

**THE GIFT FORM** — subtitle: *six axes, one tetrahedron; remove a vertex and the gift shadows into extraction.*

## Pull-quote candidates

1. A gift is not a thing transferred. A gift is the form that circulates between distinct beings without converting their relation into ledger.
2. What looks like generosity in one direction often turns out, on closer reading, to be extraction in another.
3. The shadows are not failures of intention; they are the gift form's own geometry running in reverse when one vertex drops out.
4. A gift that requires the giver's continued presence to operate is something else: dependency, performance, theater.
5. A gift form running on three vertices defaults into the failure pattern of the missing vertex — dissolved, isolated, overflowing, or ephemeral.
