# Read-notes — Consent Architecture

## Load-bearing distinction(s)
- **Gift vs. extraction.** The same surface action lands as gift or as extraction depending on whether consent holds the position. Consent is not a clause; it is the moment-by-moment agreement that determines which one the contact becomes.
- **Position held vs. shadow drifted.** Each of the four Wheel positions has a shadow that operates when the structural agreement breaks — the action looks identical, the consent has gone missing. Naming the position explicitly is what keeps it from drifting into its shadow.

## Chosen geometry
**vertex-mapped** — rendered as the Wheel of Consent: two axes crossing (who is doing / for whose benefit) produce four named quadrant positions at four luminous vertices. The article *is* a four-position matrix on a named cross; the figure has to be that cross.

Why: the whole argument is structural — four irreducible positions generated by two principles. A threshold or distinction would flatten the matrix to a single line. The cross with four glowing vertices shows the generating axes and the four positions in one read.

## Naming phrase
**Consent Architecture** — the structure that makes contact and limit work together. (Subtitle on figure: *Two axes. Four positions. Consent holds each one.*)

## Pull-quote candidates
1. Consent is not a clause. Consent is the moment-by-moment agreement that determines whether the same action lands as gift or as extraction.
2. Unnamed positions drift into their shadows. Naming the position explicitly is what lets the position hold.
3. The action looks identical on the surface; the consent has gone missing.
4. When in doubt, the body is the discriminator — the position the body recognizes as the position currently being held.
5. The default of platform-mediated contact is Taking dressed as Serving — the doer acts ostensibly for the receiver while the actual flow extracts.
