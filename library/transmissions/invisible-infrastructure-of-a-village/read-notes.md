# Read Notes — The Invisible Infrastructure of a Village

## Load-bearing distinction

**Visible infrastructure vs. invisible infrastructure.** The piece insists these are not the same and not the same size. The visible layer — roads, buildings, governance documents — is mapped, accounted for, legible to outsiders, and *the smaller portion*. The invisible layer — care patterns, tool-sharing, memory-keeping, mutual response — operates without being documented, is illegible by design, and is *what the village actually is*. The visible can be photographed and copied; the invisible cannot. A village with intact visible infrastructure and eroded invisible infrastructure looks fine from outside and is hollow from inside.

The secondary distinction (held in pull-quotes, not the pattern): committing the invisible to artifact destroys the form that carries it. Counting destroys; documenting formalizes; modeling invites extraction.

## Chosen geometry

**Layered substrate.** A thin luminous surface line over deeper, brighter, denser strata beneath it. One line why: the argument's whole shape is a depth claim — the visible is the surface, and the larger load-bearing reality runs below it, never committed to artifact. The figure must show that most of what holds the place sits beneath the line.

## Naming phrase

**THE INVISIBLE INFRASTRUCTURE** — surface above, substrate below: "what carries the place is below the line."

## Pull-quote candidates

1. The visible infrastructure is the smaller portion. The larger portion is invisible — and the invisible part is what the place actually is.
2. Committing it to artifact would destroy the form that carries it.
3. A village whose visible infrastructure is intact but whose invisible infrastructure has eroded looks fine from outside and is hollow from inside.
4. Counting them destroys them. Documenting them formalizes them. Turning them into a model invites the extraction that follows model-attention.
5. None of this appears in any formal map. None of it is governance. None of it is exchangeable. All of it is load-bearing.
