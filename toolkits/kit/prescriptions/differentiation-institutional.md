# Differentiation × Institutional

## Signature

When a new hire asks "who owns this?" the answer is two names with an "and" between them, or "it depends." Project decisions require pre-meetings to determine who decides. Role descriptions for adjacent roles overlap by sixty percent. The phrase "we all do that" appears in casual conversation about specific tasks.

## Reading

Movement 1 + **`consent_dynamics_lens.md`** ("who is doing"). Consent dynamics make role clarity a prerequisite for trust: if you do not know who is acting, you cannot grant or withhold consent meaningfully. Institutional Differentiation is the architecture that makes consent legible at scale.

## Practice

Each member writes one sentence — *"What I specifically do that no one else does."* Read aloud at the next meeting. No discussion, no improvement, no editing. Hear what is currently coherent and what isn't.

## Intervention

Find one task being done by "whoever is around." Assign it to a specific person, with a written description of what they own and what they don't. Or stop doing it. The withdrawal is from collective ownership; the gift is the named owner with the explicit scope.

## Expected resistance

*"That's not how we work — we're a team."* — Teams are made of distinct contributions. The phrase "we're a team" is doing the work of obscuring whose contribution failed when something fails.

## Signal of success

A new hire can read the org chart and the role descriptions in their first week and produce a correct map of who decides what. Their map matches the operating reality.
