The Work

The Work

Twenty years of trade, parked on a farm near Wheatland.

I build things and I fix things — welding, repairs, off-grid systems, small builds. Twenty years in the trades behind my hands. Right now most of that work lives on one farm, where the bus is parked, and the easiest way to tell you who I am is to walk you through what's standing there.

The bus

The bus is my house and my generator and my shop, all one thing. I built it out by hand. It sits on the farm and it runs the farm.

Last night I cleaned the roof and started painting it with white reflective sealer — got about three-quarters done before I called it. It'll drop the temperature inside by a lot. The front half is emptied out now. I'm building tables and workspaces into it as I go. That's the honest state of it: a thing being finished while it's already being lived in.

The power

The bus is a power station. Fifteen kilowatt-hours of battery, a ten-kilowatt inverter that's already live, and 2.6 kilowatts of solar, wired up and running. The motorcycle charges right off it.

This morning I started the day at 70% charge with the bike plugged in. It came in at 51%, and with its 1300-watt charger that's a little under 10% an hour, so by noon the bike's topped off and I dump the rest of the day's solar into AC in the bus. The solar's wired up and feeding the bus, and I'm already sharing the power with the farm. It was giving before it was even finished — that's the whole idea.

The water

I built a 12-volt pump and an accumulator tank, so the place has running water on demand. Then I built an automatic watering system right on top of it. Now the farm waters itself. My hands never have to touch it again.

That's the part I care most about. You build the thing once, and it keeps doing the job after you walk away. In a week this farm went from no pressure to running water and irrigation it didn't have before. I didn't pour a lot of hours into it. I built it so it'd run without me.

The rest of it

  • The solar biochar toilet — sanitation that runs off the sun, no hookup, no waste hauled off.
  • The hoop-house fan — a panel wired straight to a fan, moving air through the greenhouse for free.
  • The outdoor kitchen — set it up the other night and made margaritas and chicken and rice and beans, shared the food and the power around.

Most of that came from gifts and salvage. Paid once in scrap, runs free forever after.

None of this is a showroom. It's a place that's getting built, by a person, while he lives in it.

How I work

Two clean lanes, and I keep them apart. One is paid: you hire me, I build or fix the thing, you pay me cash. The other is free: I give something away, no strings, take what's useful and leave the rest. I don't blur them into "you owe me one." Free is free and paid is paid.

I lead with the small, quick work — the easy things that come together fast — and let that make room for the bigger ones. I hold a half-day. About a half-day of work a day, and that's the line. It's not laziness. It's the thing that keeps me from becoming the guy who can never say no.

If you're near Wheatland and you need something built or fixed, or you just want to come see the work standing, that's what the request page is for.
