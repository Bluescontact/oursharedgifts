Bus Mobile Crane Platform

STATUS: Infrastructure Buildout | Platform: 1995 International Genesis

Conversion of 1995 International Genesis (DT466 mechanical diesel, full frame rails, dually rear) into mobile crane platform with integrated workshop, power systems, and living quarters. Eight years of elevator construction experience applied to mobile infrastructure. Designed to support remote construction projects while providing complete autonomy.

System Overview: Three Functions, One Vehicle

This isn't a camper with tools. It's mobile infrastructure that enables single-operator construction capacity anywhere:

  1. Mobile crane platform — 3,200 lb lifting capacity with hydraulic outriggers
  2. Fabrication workshop — Welding, plasma cutting, complete tool suite
  3. Off-grid living quarters — Power, heat, water, workspace independence

The integration matters. This enables showing up at a remote site and beginning construction immediately—no crane rental, no separate housing, no grid connection required. The bus is the tool that builds the other tools.

Base Vehicle Specifications

VEHICLE

1995 International Genesis

CHASSIS

International 3900 FE

ENGINE

DT466 7.6L Turbo Diesel

FRAME

Full frame rails, dually rear

LENGTH

~30 feet (8-window configuration)

LAYOUT

Front: Living | Mid: Workshop | Rear: Crane

Why This Platform

  • Mechanical DT466: No electronic injection—field-repairable with hand tools, proven 500k+ mile longevity
  • Full frame rails: Body-on-frame construction, heavy C-channel designed for distributed loads
  • Dually rear axle: 19,000 lb capacity, stable platform for crane operations and loaded hauling
  • Shorter wheelbase: ~30 ft total length enables tight turns and rural property access
  • PTO capability: Direct engine power for hydraulic systems (crane, outriggers, leveling)
  • Cost: $4,000 acquisition vs $20k-50k for equivalent commercial service truck

System 1: Mobile Crane Platform

Crane Specifications (Target)

  • Model: Auto Crane 3203PRX (or equivalent)
  • Lift capacity: 3,200 lbs at 12 ft reach
  • Maximum reach: 15 feet
  • Rotation: 360 degrees when deployed
  • Power: Electric-over-hydraulic (no PTO required)
  • Integration: Runs from vehicle electrical system

Mounting Strategy

Crane mounts approximately 6 feet from rear wall. Mounting frame distributes forces across 10-15 feet of bus frame rails, turning entire frame into one leg of the stability system rather than concentrating stress at single point.

  • Stowed position: Crane lies flat on removable roof rack section, weight centered over rear axle
  • Deployed position: Crane stands up with full 360° rotation. First task: lift off own roof cover
  • Load distribution: 10-15 ft frame engagement distributes moment loads
  • Outrigger integration: Hydraulic outriggers at rear corners transfer crane loads to ground

Hydraulic Outriggers

Hydraulic outriggers deploy at rear corners when crane operates. This transfers loads to ground rather than relying on suspension, creating stable platform for lifting operations.

  • Deployment sequence: Level chassis first, then extend outriggers to lock position
  • Pad size: 18"×18" minimum for New Mexico soil conditions
  • Integration: Hydraulic pump runs from vehicle electrical or engine-driven
  • Design target: 2-ton lifting capacity with outriggers deployed

What This Enables

  • Single-operator construction: Lifting capacity equivalent to 4-person crew
  • Remote building: No crane rental infrastructure required
  • Material handling: Lumber, panels, equipment, structures
  • Aerial rigging: Mobile anchor point for circus arts practice

System 2: Mobile Workshop

Mid-section configured as complete fabrication shop. Not just tool storage—actual workshop capability with everything required to build structures from raw materials.

Workshop Equipment (On Hand)

  • Welding: MIG, stick, plasma cutting
  • Power tools: Saws, drills, grinders, impact drivers
  • Hand tools: Complete mechanic's set, specialty tools
  • Materials stock: Steel plate (1/16", 1/8"), tubing, aluminum extrusion
  • Work surfaces: Welding bench, assembly table
  • Storage: Organized tool cabinets, parts bins

Workshop Layout Strategy

  • Fixed installations: Welding equipment cabinet, grinder stands, vise mounting
  • Secured storage: Everything strapped/bolted for road travel
  • Power access: 120V and 240V outlets throughout workspace
  • Ventilation: Roof vents, exhaust fans for welding fumes
  • Workflow: Material storage → cutting → fabrication → finishing

What This Enables

  • On-site fabrication: Build custom components where they're needed
  • Repair capability: Fix broken equipment without returning to shop
  • Training delivery: Teach welding/fabrication at deployment sites
  • Infrastructure on demand: The workshop builds the other tools

System 3: Power & Living Systems

Electrical System

  • Battery bank: 30kWh lithium (current: 10kWh, expanding to 30kWh)
  • Inverter: 10kW continuous output
  • Solar array: 400W initial, expandable to 800W+
  • Backup generation: 5kW diesel/multi-fuel generator
  • Distribution: 120V AC throughout, 240V exterior outlets

Power System Design Philosophy

This isn't RV electrical—it's workshop-capable mobile power platform. 30kWh storage provides 4+ days autonomy at modest use. 5kW generator can top up from dead to full in ~6 hours, enabling extended off-grid work or welding operations without solar input.

Power budget (daily):

  • Living systems: LED lighting, laptop, phone charging, fans ~1.5 kWh/day
  • Climate control: Diesel heater 30-50W continuous when running
  • Workshop loads: Power tools, welding equipment as needed
  • Crane operation: Hydraulic pump from battery or generator

Climate Control

  • Heating: Diesel heater (2-5kW output, thermostat controlled)
  • Fuel use: 0.1-0.2 gal/day diesel = $3-6/day in winter
  • Cooling: Roof vents, 12V fans, awning for shade
  • Insulation: R-13 walls, R-19 ceiling, thermal curtains on windows

Water Systems

  • Fresh water: IBC tote-based storage with 12V pump
  • Hot water: Propane or electric heater
  • Interior: Sink, shower capability
  • Greywater: Collection for disposal or irrigation

Living Space Configuration

Forward section (front 15-20 feet) becomes habitable living quarters. Not luxury—functional shelter for extended remote work.

  • Sleeping area: Built-in bed platform with storage underneath
  • Kitchen: Sink, propane cooktop, small fridge
  • Workspace: Desk for computer work, planning, documentation
  • Storage: Cabinets, shelving, gear organization

System Integration: How It All Works Together

Operational Sequence

1. ARRIVE at remote site
2. DEPLOY outriggers → level platform
3. EXTEND solar array → start battery charging
4. RAISE crane → begin lifting operations
5. FABRICATE components in workshop as needed
6. OPERATE crane for installation
7. LIVE on-site for project duration
8. STOW systems → move to next site

Power Flow

  • Solar → batteries: Continuous charging when available
  • Batteries → inverter: Clean 120V/240V AC power
  • Generator backup: Extended cloudy periods or high-draw welding
  • Shore power integration: When available, supplements/replaces solar

Material Flow

  • Storage → crane: Lift heavy materials from cargo area
  • Workshop → crane: Fabricated components to installation point
  • Ground → structure: Crane positions elements for assembly

Why This Matters

This project demonstrates integration of construction capacity with mobile living platform. Most people need a crane rental for major building projects—expensive and availability-limited in remote areas. This approach makes crane capability mobile and owner-operated.

The documentation enables others to develop mobile construction platforms suited to their needs. Not everyone needs a crane, but the principles—frame integration, power systems, workshop organization, living space optimization—apply to any conversion that combines work capacity with nomadic living.

Eight years of elevator construction taught me that most building problems are rigging problems. The bus makes rigging capacity mobile. Everything else follows from that.
